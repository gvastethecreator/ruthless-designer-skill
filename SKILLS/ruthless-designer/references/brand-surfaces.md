# Brand And Persuasion Surfaces

Use this route for landing pages, launches, portfolios, pricing, campaigns, services, commerce storytelling, and cultural/editorial public surfaces whose design must persuade, express identity, or reward discovery.

## Solve The Argument First

Identify:

- Offer: what is sold, launched, explained, or requested.
- Audience: who evaluates it and what pressure they feel.
- Intended response: signup, contact, purchase, upgrade, demo, download, subscribe, explore, remember, attend, or trust.
- Proof: product artifact, screenshot, output, case, metric, testimonial, customer, security, or example.
- Objections: price, complexity, migration, lock-in, risk, time-to-value, support, or trust.
- Constraints: brand assets, SEO, analytics, legal copy, forms, IA, and available media.

Build page structure from the offer, proof, and objections. A stock section order is not strategy; it is a template wearing your nouns.

## Diagnose Information And Content

Before styling, map the audience's questions in decision order: `what is this -> is it for me -> how does it work -> why believe it -> what does it cost/risk -> what next`. Inspect navigation labels, page titles, claim/proof adjacency, repeated ideas, missing objections, buried terms, and whether the content model has a real home for every important object.

Cut duplication, merge synonymous sections, rewrite vague labels as domain actions, and move proof beside the claim it supports. If the page needs three paragraphs to explain its own navigation, the content architecture is the corpse; typography is only doing the makeup.

Mystery and delayed reveal are valid for cultural, editorial, entertainment, and discovery surfaces when anticipation is part of the value. Preserve orientation, access, skip/exit, reduced motion, and a clear path for users who came with a concrete task. Confusion is not atmosphere.

## First Viewport

For commercial, service, and conversion-led surfaces, make the first viewport carry:

- A specific offer or product/category signal.
- A concise support line.
- One dominant action.
- One credibility signal.
- A visible hint of the next proof, product, or mechanism section.

For cultural, editorial, entertainment, and discovery surfaces, make it carry identity, one orientation cue, an intentional entry into the artifact or story, and reachable wayfinding. A dominant CTA, support line, or credibility badge is optional when it would flatten earned mystery into funnel sludge.

Keep it legible on a small laptop and mobile. Kill the four-line display headline, tag soup, secondary CTA shouting match, decorative metadata, and hero art that hides what the product does.

Prefer a real artifact, screenshot, object, place, output, person, material, or interaction. If no credible asset exists, state the gap. Do not counterfeit a product with nested divs and call the forgery proof.

## Page Rhythm

Choose only the section jobs the intended response needs; this is a palette, not a mandatory funnel:

- Claim and immediate evidence.
- Product mechanism or workflow.
- Comparison: old/new, manual/automated, current/target, plan/plan.
- Outcome or case.
- Objection and trust response.
- Pricing or fit decision.
- Final action at the point of highest conviction.
- Encounter, fragment, context, sequence, archive/programme, or return path for cultural and editorial discovery.

Do not force pricing, objection handling, testimonials, or repeated conversion into a surface whose job is exploration, remembrance, attendance, or cultural encounter. It still needs orientation and access; it does not need to cosplay as quarterly-growth software.

Use an artifact stage, proof wall, comparison canvas, editorial path, or product-led landing structure when it strengthens the argument. Avoid repeating equal icon-heading-paragraph cards, alternating text/image bands with no rhythm change, and the same eyebrow over every section.

Place proof before decoration and conversion where proof peaks. A CTA repeated every 400 pixels does not create conviction; it advertises desperation.

## Pricing

Lock the model before drawing cards: per-seat, usage, tiered, flat, freemium, enterprise, or custom.

- Align plan names, price, period, limits, CTA, and comparable feature rows.
- Explain who the recommended plan is for and why.
- Expose overages, taxes, currencies, billing period, custom quote, and hidden limits.
- Keep risk reversal—trial, cancellation, migration, SLA, support, refund—near the decision.
- Use tabular numerals for changing prices.
- Use tooltips or disclosure for complex limits without hiding material terms.

Organize by fit and consequence, not a wall of checkmarks.

## Dark-Pattern Veto

Never earn conversion through confusion or exhaustion. Kill prechecked consent, hidden fees or material limits, fake scarcity, misleading countdowns, confirmshaming, disguised ads, forced continuity, obstructed cancellation, visual demotion of the honest choice, and refusal loops that ask the same question until the user surrenders.

Make price, renewal, consequence, data use, and reversibility legible before commitment. The design must survive informed choice. A conversion rate purchased with deception is not persuasion; it is evidence that the interface learned mugging.

## Reference-Led Work

Use screenshots, URLs, videos, HTML exports, brand assets, and generated section comps when they reduce guessing. Capture the narrowest useful evidence:

- Screenshot/image: hierarchy, grid, type scale, palette roles, radius, shadow, media treatment, copy density, and signature detail.
- URL/page: relevant viewport and section crops; do not trust blank full-page captures when lazy load, canvas, or scroll effects interfere.
- Video: inspect beats—purpose, layout, motion, interaction, timing, and implementation risk.
- Inspiration set: use a few references with a reason for each, not a compressed moodboard graveyard.

Record source, role, extracted system, constraints, what not to copy, and proof gaps. Convert observations into tokens, components, layout rules, and proof targets. Copy the system logic, not the costume.

## Visual Voice

- Choose type from an audience/offer axis, not fashion. Use contrast deliberately; keep long display copy below billboard scale.
- Derive palette from a real brand artifact, scene, product material, or image. Name roles before values.
- Choose one committed visual constraint and one signature move. More effects do not create more identity.
- Give media deliberate crop, edge, caption, and inspection affordance.
- Use asymmetric section roles and varied rhythm when the story requires them.
- Use literal, domain-specific copy: object, action, audience, and consequence.

Kill reflexive purple-blue gradients, beige/brass luxury, decorative glass, terminal cosplay, faux brutalism, default editorial monochrome, tiny uppercase kickers, fake coordinates/version strips, random serif italics, and generic "empower/streamline/supercharge" claims.

These are not universal bans. They are guilty until the product provides an alibi.

## Immersive Effects

Use canvas, WebGL, 3D, particles, physics, globes, or scroll storytelling only when the effect is a real artifact, mechanism, place, data object, or requested brand experience. Prefer the existing stack. Add a dependency only when static media or CSS cannot carry the idea.

Require foreground readability, reduced motion, mobile/static fallback, offscreen pause, device-pixel/density caps, cleanup/disposal, and runtime proof. A shader that burns the laptop to decorate a vague claim is not immersive; it is arson.

## Pass Gate

Pass only when:

- The page answers—or deliberately stages—what it is, who it is for, why to trust or enter it, and what happens next; concrete-task users retain a reachable direct path.
- Real artifact, context, or proof appears when the experience promises it, and each section has a distinct persuasive or experiential role.
- The first viewport survives small laptop and mobile.
- The signature move is visible, useful, and specific to the offer, artifact, story, or intended response.
- Applicable SEO, analytics, legal, forms, rights, and accessibility constraints remain intact.
- References and assets are named, verified, and compared against the result.
